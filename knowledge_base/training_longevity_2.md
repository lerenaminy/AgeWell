HIIT workouts, strength training, easy runs, and mobility sessions can all help you stay healthier and live longer.

## The Workout Types You Need on Your Schedule to Boost Longevity

Training for longevity is about fitting in different forms of movement so you can keep doing what you love for as long as possible. Research has shown speed workouts, low-intensity cardio, strength training, and mobility, can all increase longevity.

“The idea is to balance the different intensities throughout your day, your week, or even throughout your monthly training program,” says Jan Schroder, PhD, founder of Garage Girls Fitness and professor in the Department of Kinesiology at Long Beach State University.

But with so many forms of movement to choose from, how do you fit them all into your schedule?

We spoke with the experts to find out. Plus, we created four weekly workout schedules, designed for different goals and experience levels.

### High-Intensity/Speed Workouts

The CDC recommends at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity physical activity or 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity activity each week, or a combination of both. This can include interval workouts, tempo runs, zone 2 miles, and even cross-training.

But our experts agree if you’re training for longevity, you should do at least one workout per week at high intensity, meaning it pushes your heart rate into zones 4 to 5 or above your lactate threshold. This means you’re working at an effort that doesn’t allow you to speak in full sentences.

High-intensity workouts are important because they help improve your cardiovascular system, your muscular system, and your body’s ability to take up oxygen (particularly your VO2 max), all which lead to a reduction in all-cause mortality when done consistently, says Phil Batterson, PhD, head physiologist, coach, and founder of Critical Oxygen Labs. “VO2 max is a very good predictor of all-cause mortality, meaning that the higher your VO2 max is, the lower your chance of dying,” Batterson says.

Batterson refers to a study published in June 2025 in Sports Medicine that suggests exercise above zone 2 is important for maximizing cardiometabolic health benefits. “At the end of the day, it’s the high-intensity stuff that is really going to make the biggest difference for your overall health,” he says.

The key is doing about one high-intensity session per week and matching your efforts to your fitness level. For example, beginners can start out with a simple fartlek workout like alternating one minute of a hard, high-intensity run and one minute of jogging recovery for five rounds. A more advanced version of this would look repeating the entire five-round fartlek for another set, so you do 10 rounds total.

### Low-Intensity Cardio

While it’s smart to have higher intensity efforts on your calendar, you should still round out your schedule with more zone 2 work, whether that’s via easy runs, the run/walk method, or cross-training. This will not only allow you to work out more often without risking injury, but will allow you to move more throughout the day, which is key to longevity, says Kylie Morgan, ACSM-certified clinical exercise physiologist, UESCA-certified run, and strength coach. “We, as a whole, don’t move enough as a society. We want people to move more,” she says.

Many studies have shown that increasing your daily physical activity leads to a longer life expectancy. For example, Runner’s World previously reported on a study from the British Journal of Medicine that suggests walking for 160 minutes a day could increase life expectancy by about five years. “Just move more. Don’t be sedentary,” says Batterson.

In addition to simply walking more throughout the day, each expert recommends using zone 2 runs and cross-training to fill in your easy workout days, aiming for about three or more, depending on your fitness level and goals.

Schroeder suggests dedicating at least one to three days to rest and active recovery, too. “Recovery is when you blossom,” she says. “That is when the body recovers and rebuilds stronger than what it was before.”

Active recovery doesn’t just have to look like walking or easy runs. It can also include active hobbies like gardening, hiking, or completing chores around the house, Batterson adds.

### Strength Training

In addition to cardio exercise, the CDC recommendation also suggests adults strength train at least two days per week. And science backs up why.

Research shows that combining aerobic training with strength training can help reduce your risk of dying from illnesses, including cardiovascular disease, cancer, Alzheimer’s, and diabetes by 40 percent. And a study published in 2024 in Biology suggests strength training for 90 minutes a week could lower your cellular age by four years.

Schroeder recommends lifting heavy weights two to four times a week, but even once is better than nothing, she says.

Each expert agrees strength training is crucial as you get older to prevent loss of muscle mass (a.k.a. sarcopenia). This allows you to maintain mobility as you get older, which can prevent you from falling. The more strength and mobility you build throughout your lifetime, the more likely you are to keep it in your later years, says Schroeder.

Strength training and plyometrics also supports bone density, according to research, which helps prevent osteoporosis and osteopenia as you get older, Morgan adds.

## 4 Sample Weekly Training Schedules for Longevity

Training for longevity is all about consistency, Morgan explains, and that starts with mapping out what you can do each week.

Schroder and Morgan both suggest five days of mobility work a week, but strength workouts can count toward that number. They suggest implementing a 10- to 20-minute mobility routine on days you don’t strength train.

Morgan explains that balancing different types of workouts with a variety of heart rate zones is crucial for longevity, as you can reap the benefits of each type of workout.
