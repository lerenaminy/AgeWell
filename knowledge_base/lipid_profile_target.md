Lipid targets focus on reducing Cardiovascular Disease (CVD) risk, with LDL-C as the primary target and ApoB as a preferred secondary target for residual risk. Goals vary by risk: high-risk individuals should aim for LDL-C <70 mg/dL and ApoB <80 mg/dL, while very high-risk patients target LDL-C <55 mg/dL and ApoB <65 mg/dL.

## LDL-C (Primary Target)
Very High Risk (e.g., prior heart attack): <55 mg/dL (<1.4 mmol/L).  
High Risk: <70 mg/dL (<1.8 mmol/L).  
Moderate Risk: <100 mg/dL (<2.6 mmol/L).

## ApoB (Key Secondary Target)
Very High Risk: <65 mg/dL.  
High Risk: <80 mg/dL.  
General/Moderate Risk: <90 mg/dL.

Note: ApoB is increasingly used because it directly measures the number of atherogenic particles (LDL + VLDL + IDL), providing a better marker than LDL-C alone.

## Triglycerides (Trigs)
Optimal: <150 mg/dL (<1.7 mmol/L).  
Borderline High: 150–199 mg/dL.  
High triglycerides are often accompanied by higher ApoB and smaller, denser LDL particles, increasing cardiovascular risk.

## Clinical Context
If a patient has high triglycerides, metabolic syndrome, or diabetes, ApoB is a superior, more reliable target for measuring the true risk of cardiovascular events compared to LDL-C alone.
